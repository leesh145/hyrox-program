Injury modification
Clinical rationale: Warm-up is a non-negotiable injury mitigation step given your profile. Each session type has a targeted sequence. Do not skip or abbreviate — with T10–12 facet involvement and right lower limb instability, cold loading is the highest-risk scenario.
Day A — Strength Day (15–18 min total)
1
General elevation — Bike or Rower
5 min @ RPE 3–4. Nasal breathing only. No effort — this is tissue warming, not a training stimulus. Gradually increase cadence toward minute 4.
5 min
2
Thoracic foam roller mobilisation
Foam roller at T8–T10. Support head with hands. Extend gently over roller in 5 × 5-second holds. Move roller one segment toward T12 and repeat. This is gentle mobilisation — not aggressive cracking. Do this before axial loading, not after.
3–4 min
3
Hip flexor / psoas stretch
Half-kneeling position, 45s each side. Add posterior pelvic tilt at end-range. This decompresses the thoracolumbar junction and releases hip flexor tension before loaded squatting movements.
2 min
4
Ankle CARs (Controlled Articular Rotations) mod
Seated, right ankle: slow full-range circumduction, 10 rotations each direction. Then 10 × slow dorsiflexion/plantarflexion. This restores synovial fluid distribution and wakes up the proprioceptive pathway before any loading.
2 min
5
Glute activation sequence
Banded clamshells: 2 × 12 each side. Banded glute bridge: 2 × 10. This activates the glute med and max before any squat or hinge pattern — especially important given right hamstring atrophy and the compensatory glute inhibition that typically follows.
3 min
6
Movement prep — Day A specific
2 × 5 bodyweight goblet squats (slow, to depth), 2 × 5 RDL with empty barbell or light kettlebell. Establish movement pattern before adding load. Check for symmetry in hamstring tension left vs right.
2 min
Day B — Conditioning Day (12–14 min total)
1
Easy ski erg or bike — arms-only focus
5 min @ RPE 3. In week 1, begin every ski erg session with 60 seconds of arms-only skiing to establish the pull-through pattern before adding leg drive. Release the handle at hip height — pulling through to full hip extension risks T10–12 compressive extension at end-range.
5 min
2
Dynamic movement prep
Walking hip circles × 10 each, leg swings (front-to-back + side-to-side) × 10 each, high knees × 20m, butt kicks × 20m. Raise core temperature and lubricate the hip joints before any running or sled work.
4 min
3
Right ankle proprioception activation mod
Single-leg stance on right foot, eyes open: 3 × 20 seconds. Progress to eyes closed once stable. Do this before any running or sled push — it pre-activates the peroneal stabilisers and dramatically reduces ankle re-injury risk.
2–3 min
4
Sled or carry movement prep
If sled is programmed: 1 × 10m push with an empty or near-empty sled before your working sets. Establish posture — forward lean from ankles, not waist. Brace the thoracic spine and maintain neutral extension throughout.
2 min
Day C — Hybrid Day (10–12 min total)
1
Easy 3–4 min jog
Conversational pace. Land midfoot. If the right ankle feels stiff, shorten to 2 min and add more ankle mobility before progressing. Do not run through pre-existing stiffness that hasn't resolved — it's a load amplifier.
3–4 min
2
Open books — thoracic rotation mod
Side-lying open books: 10 each side, slow and controlled. This is the primary pre-session thoracic mobility drill for the T10–12 segment. If right rotation is restricted or painful, do not load the thoracic spine that session until mobility improves post warm-up.
3 min
3
Ankle and hip mobility flow
World's greatest stretch × 5 each side (includes ankle dorsiflexion component). Lateral lunge with ankle reach × 8 each side. These address the combined hip/ankle demand of the hybrid session's varied movements.
3 min
4
Session-specific movement prep
5 × wall balls @ sub-maximal height, 1 × easy farmers carry 15m, 1 × ski erg 30s easy. Run through the day's movements at low intensity before your working sets begin.
2 min
Clinical context: These modifications are not optional progressions — they are load management decisions based on your injury history. They remain in place at all training intensities. The right lower limb chain and T10–12 segment are treated as sentinel areas throughout all 8 weeks.
Right Ankle
Ankle CARs every sessionPerformed during warm-up (seated, full circumduction × 10 each direction + 10 × dorsiflexion/plantarflexion) before any loading.
Single-leg balance pre-activationEyes-open single-leg stance on right foot before every session: 3 × 20s. Pre-activates peroneal stabilisers. Progress to eyes-closed from week 3 if stable.
No unilateral plyometric loadingNo single-leg jumps, box jumps, or bounding on the right side throughout. Bilateral only until proprioception is rated stable at the week 3 check.
Tape or brace for running sessionsUse rigid strapping tape or a lace-up ankle brace for all running and sled days. Not optional in weeks 1–4. Reassess at week 5.
Running surface preferenceFlat, even surfaces only. Avoid trail, grass, or cambered surfaces through the first four weeks. Treadmill is acceptable for interval work.
Pre-symptomatic responseIf the ankle feels "off" pre-session — reduce unilateral loading and cut run volume 50% that day. Do not push through developing symptoms.
Right Knee / Hamstring
B-stance and left-lead bias (Phase 1)All bilateral lower body exercises use a left-leg-lead stance in weeks 1–2. Right limb participates but carries less load. B-stance RDL, single-leg hip thrust left lead.
Nordic curl negatives only (Phase 1–2)Begin Phase 1 with 2 × 5 eccentric-only Nordic curls (controlled 4-second lower, partner or foot anchor). Do not attempt the concentric phase until right hamstring symmetry test passes consistently.
Bulgarian split squat — depth managementRight leg behind: reduce ROM by approximately 20% compared to left. Watch for right knee caving medially on descent — if present, reduce depth further and add VMO terminal knee extensions (banded TKEs) as a correction drill.
No loaded knee flexion beyond 90° (Phase 1)Leg curls, heavy lunges, and deep step-ups are excluded in weeks 1–2. Introduce cautiously from week 3 only if hamstring symmetry is improving.
Right hamstring palpation checkAfter every strength session: palpate the right proximal hamstring (ischial tuberosity insertion). Any focal tenderness = modify next session and do not progress the Nordic protocol.
Post-session asymmetry responseIf right hamstring is significantly more sore than left post-session: reduce hamstring loading across all sessions that week. Do not progress until symmetry returns.
T10–12 Facet
Daily thoracic mobilisation before every sessionFoam roller extension at T8–T12: 5 × 5-second holds per segment, moving caudally. Always done when tissue is cold — this segment mobilises best before loading, not after.
Seated cable row priorityAll horizontal rowing uses seated cable or chest-supported incline bench position. No unsupported bent-over rows — the lumbar and thoracic loading angle creates compressive shear at the facet joints.
Farmers carry — load managementModerate loads only, never near-maximal. If localised T10–12 pain occurs during any carry, set the load down immediately. Do not walk through thoracic pain under axial load.
Ski erg — technique disciplineRelease the handle at hip height. Pulling through to full hip extension risks compressive extension at T10–12 end-range. In week 1, arms-only skiing for the first 60s of every interval before adding leg drive.
Pre-session ache protocolDull T10–12 ache pre-session: reduce all axial loading by 30% and skip sled that session. Substitute stationary bike or rower as the cardio element — these place significantly lower demand on the thoracic segment.
Referred pain = stop immediatelyIf pain refers around the ribcage, into the abdomen, or produces any lower limb neurological symptom — stop immediately and seek same-day clinical review. This is not a "train through it" scenario.
Decision framework: These signals are calibrated to your specific injury profile. In the absence of injury history, some of these "stop" signals might be assessed differently. With right lower limb instability, hamstring atrophy, and facet involvement active, the threshold for stopping or modifying is appropriately lower than a healthy athlete profile.
STOP — Seek clinical review before your next session
Any radicular or referred pain originating from the thoracic region — around the ribcage, into the abdomen, or toward the lower limbs.
Focal right hamstring tenderness at the ischial tuberosity insertion that begins during or after running (not DOMS — sharp, localised onset).
Right ankle swelling during or within 2 hours of completing a session.
Sharp, catching, or locking sensation in the right knee under any loaded movement.
Any symptom rated 7/10 or above on your subjective pain scale during exercise that is not clearly bilateral and effort-related.
Neurological symptoms at any point: numbness, tingling, or weakness in either lower limb.
MODIFY — Reduce load, substitute, and monitor closely
Right hamstring asymmetry post-session: Right hamstring significantly more sore than left after a strength day — reduce hamstring loading 30–40% across all sessions that week. Do not progress the Nordic curl protocol.
Dull pre-session T10–12 ache: Reduce all axial loading 30% for the session. Skip sled. Substitute bike or rower for any conditioning cardio element. Perform an extended thoracic foam roll sequence before proceeding.
Pre-symptomatic right ankle: Ankle feels "off," hypersensitive, or unstable pre-session — tape it, reduce unilateral loading to zero, and cut planned run volume by 50%. Continue bilateral conditioning only.
Right knee medial tracking pain on descent: Reduce squat/split squat depth immediately. Add 2 × 15 VMO terminal knee extensions (banded TKEs) as corrective work at the end of the session. Assess cause (ankle compensation, glute weakness) before next session.
HRV/HR under-recovery signal: Resting HR 5–7+ bpm above baseline or HRV 10%+ below 7-day average — downgrade entire session to technique and mobility work only. Do not push training load against accumulated fatigue with this injury profile.
CONTINUE — Normal training response, proceed as programmed
Bilateral posterior chain DOMS (glutes, hamstrings) 24–48 hours after strength days. Symmetric soreness is a positive adaptation signal.
General fatigue and elevated HR during the first 3–4 minutes of conditioning sessions, especially weeks 1–3.
Mild thoracic morning stiffness that resolves fully within 20 minutes of movement and warm-up.
Mild bilateral quad and glute soreness after heavy lower body weeks (4–5). This is normal progressive overload response.
HR taking longer to recover between intervals in weeks 3–5 — this is a positive aerobic adaptation under accumulated fatigue. It resolves as conditioning improves.
General training-day fatigue that is fully resolved within 24 hours and does not compound week-to-week.
When to perform: Before your first session of every week. Takes 5 minutes. Run through all four checks before making any load decisions for that week. Your self-assessment background makes you well-placed to use these accurately.
CHECK 01 / 04
Right Hamstring Symmetry Test
Perform 5 bodyweight single-leg RDLs on each side. Assess: does the right feel significantly weaker, less stable, or produce posterior thigh discomfort compared to the left? Note the quality of the hip hinge — is the right pelvis dropping or rotating compared to the left?
PASS → Proceed with programmed hamstring loading for the week
FAIL → Reduce hamstring loading that week. Do not progress Nordic curl. Reassess next week.
CHECK 02 / 04
Right Ankle Proprioception Check
30-second single-leg stance on the right foot, eyes closed. Count the number of touch-down corrections. By week 3, you should complete this with minimal to no corrections. This is not just a balance test — it's a real-time read on the neuromuscular status of the peroneal stabiliser chain.
PASS → Proprioceptive progression on track. Maintain current prehab volume.
FAIL (past week 3) → Proprioceptive regression. Add one extra prehab set before every session that week. Do not increase single-leg loading until the check passes.
CHECK 03 / 04
Thoracic Rotation Screen
Seated upright on a flat chair, arms crossed on your chest. Rotate fully to the left, then fully to the right. Assess: is range approximately equal? Is there pain, catching, or sharp sensation at end-range on either side? Restricted or painful right rotation implicates the right T10–12 facet — the primary at-risk segment for your injury history.
PASS → Proceed with normal axial loading for the session.
FAIL → No axial loading that session until mobility improves during warm-up foam rolling. If restriction persists through warm-up: substitute bike/rower for all loaded spinal movements and review with your physio if it occurs two weeks in a row.
CHECK 04 / 04
Resting HR / HRV (if you use a wearable)
Check your resting heart rate and HRV first thing in the morning before session 1 of each week. A resting HR more than 5–7 bpm above your personal baseline, or an HRV more than 10% below your 7-day rolling average, is a meaningful under-recovery signal. Accumulated fatigue with your injury profile is a genuine risk amplifier — this is load management science, not a rest-day mindset.
PASS → Normal recovery status. Proceed as programmed.
FAIL → Downgrade that session to technique and mobility work only. Do not push training load. Reassess the next day before deciding whether to skip or proceed.
